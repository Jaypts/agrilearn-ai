The problem

In Uganda, agricultural extension officers — the trained staff who normally help farmers identify crop problems — are stretched far too thin to reach most smallholder farms regularly. By the time a farmer can get expert eyes on a sick plant, a treatable problem can already be spreading. Most farmers, even in remote areas, now carry a basic smartphone — that camera is the fastest expert access point available.

How AgriLearn actually works

AgriLearn AI does not run a single black-box guess. Each diagnosis goes through two layers: (1) an independent pixel-level color analysis runs in the browser — no AI, just HSV color-space thresholding — to measure what % of the leaf is green, yellowing, or necrotic; (2) that measurement is fed into the prompt for a multimodal AI model (Gemini 3.5 Flash) as a numeric cross-check against its own visual read, with automatic retries if the connection drops mid-request. This is an honest design choice: it doesn't replace a properly trained, validated classifier, but it's a real multi-signal check rather than trusting one raw model guess with no cross-reference.

Where it's strong
  1. Explains why a disease happens, not just its name — this is the educational core of the app.
  2. Shows its reasoning ("what we saw") and an independent pixel-color measurement, so a farmer can sanity-check the diagnosis against their own eyes and the numbers.
  3. Retries automatically on a dropped connection instead of failing outright.
  4. Works in three languages, and reads results aloud for low-literacy users.
  5. Compresses photos before sending, so it's usable on limited mobile data.
  6. Tracks real farmer-confirmed accuracy over time (Field Journal tab) — not a claimed number, an actual running tally from outcome feedback.
Where it's honestly limited
  1. Confidence scores are still ultimately model self-assessment, not a lab-calibrated statistic — treat anything under 60% as a starting guess, not a verdict.
  2. The pixel-color analysis is a coarse heuristic (HSV thresholding) — it can be fooled by soil, glare, or background foliage in the frame, which is why it's shown as a cross-check, not a standalone verdict.
  3. It hasn't yet been validated against a large labelled dataset or by a trained agronomist — that's the most important next step before any real-money treatment decisions should be based on it.
  4. It depends on an internet connection per diagnosis; the Learn Library is the offline fallback by design.
What shipping this for real would need next

Validation against confirmed cases with local agricultural officers, an SMS/USSD channel for farmers without smartphones, and a feedback loop where farmers report whether a diagnosis turned out correct — turning every use of the app into a small piece of real-world accuracy data.
